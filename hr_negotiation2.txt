KNOWLEDGE BASE: HR Salary Negotiation — Extended FAQ (30 Questions)
====================================================================

Q01: How do I open a salary negotiation without sounding aggressive?
A: Start with curiosity, not confrontation. Say: "Before we discuss numbers, I'd love to
   understand what's most important to you in this role — is it the fixed component, total
   package, growth, or something else?" This shifts the conversation from positional
   bargaining to interest-based negotiation and gives you valuable anchoring information.

Q02: The candidate says "I have another offer." How do I respond without panicking?
A: Stay calm and ask: "That's great — congratulations. Can I ask, if both offers were
   identical on paper, which role would you choose?" If they say yours, you've won on
   value — now close the gap on numbers. If they say the other, find out why and address
   that reason directly. Never match blindly without knowing what you're competing on.

Q03: How do I negotiate with a candidate who refuses to disclose their current salary?
A: Don't push. Instead, anchor from your side: "Our band for this role is ₹X to ₹Y.
   Does that range work for you?" This puts the frame on your terms without requiring
   their disclosure. In many regions, asking for current salary is also legally restricted.

Q04: A candidate accepted verbally but is now asking for more. What do I do?
A: Acknowledge first: "I appreciate you coming back to me before we signed documents."
   Then hold firm unless there's new information: "Our offer was based on the full picture
   we discussed. Has something changed that I should know about?" Only reopen if there's
   a genuine new factor — competing offer, relocation costs, etc.

Q05: How do I negotiate with a very senior candidate (VP/Director level)?
A: Senior candidates negotiate on total wealth creation, not monthly salary. Lead with
   ESOP quantum and vesting schedule, board-level visibility, and long-term incentive
   plans. Salary is often secondary. Frame it as: "Let me show you the full wealth
   creation picture over a 4-year horizon."

Q06: What do I say when a candidate asks: "Is this your best offer?"
A: Be honest but strategic. If it is: "Yes, this is the maximum I'm authorised to offer.
   I want to be respectful of your time." If it isn't: "This is our standard offer for
   this band. If there are specific elements you'd like to discuss, I'm open to that
   conversation." Never lie — candidates remember, and it damages trust.

Q07: How do I handle a candidate who keeps moving the goalposts?
A: Name the pattern gently: "I want to make sure we reach a place that works for both
   of us. Could we agree on what 'yes' looks like for you today, so I know what to
   work toward?" This forces them to commit to a clear condition, preventing endless
   movement. If they can't state a condition, they may not be serious.

Q08: A candidate says their spouse/family needs them to earn more. How do I respond?
A: Validate empathetically: "I completely understand — this is a family decision, not
   just a professional one." Then pivot to total value: "Let me show you the full
   financial picture including the health cover, ESOP potential, and performance
   bonus, so your family has the complete number to evaluate."

Q09: How do I negotiate with a freshly promoted candidate who now has a higher title?
A: Their new title may not yet have the market premium at their current employer. Ask:
   "Has your compensation been revised to reflect the new title?" If not, point out:
   "Our offer already prices in the seniority level — you'd be stepping in fully valued
   from day one, rather than waiting for your next appraisal cycle."

Q10: What's the best way to handle silence after making an offer?
A: Let the silence sit for 5–7 seconds. Resist the urge to fill it or sweeten the offer
   unprompted. If silence extends beyond 10 seconds, ask: "What's going through your
   mind right now?" This invites them to share their concern rather than you guessing
   and over-conceding.

Q11: How do I convince a candidate to relocate for a lower salary than they expected?
A: Quantify the relocation support: lump-sum allowance, temporary housing, school
   admission support if they have children. Then compare cost of living: "₹20L in
   Bangalore has the purchasing power of ₹26L in Mumbai — your real standard of
   living actually improves." Use a cost-of-living index to make this concrete.

Q12: The candidate's ask is within budget but at the top. Should I offer the maximum?
A: No — always leave room. Start at 85–90% of your max. This gives you headroom to
   move if they push back, and candidates who accept the first offer sometimes feel
   they left money on the table, which creates early regret. A slight negotiation
   actually increases offer satisfaction.

Q13: How do I explain why our salary is below market without sounding defensive?
A: Reframe the comparison: "Our fixed pay is calibrated at the 55th percentile of
   market, by design — we make up the difference through higher variable potential,
   ESOP, and a faster promotion cycle. We find this attracts people who are confident
   in their performance." This turns a weakness into a self-selection signal.

Q14: A mid-level candidate is asking for a C-suite level salary. How do I handle it?
A: Don't dismiss — explore: "Help me understand how you arrived at that number. Are
   you benchmarking against a specific role or company?" Often it's based on a friend's
   story or incorrect data. Share your benchmarking source calmly. If they hold firm
   unreasonably, a graceful exit is better than forcing a mismatch.

Q15: How do I negotiate with a candidate who is currently unemployed?
A: Be respectful — don't exploit their situation. Exploiting urgency creates resentment
   and early attrition. Make a fair offer and be transparent: "We're not pricing this
   based on your current situation — we're pricing it for the role and your skills."
   Candidates who feel treated fairly are more loyal.

Q16: How do I close a negotiation that has been going back and forth for two weeks?
A: Set a deadline with empathy: "I want to give you the time you need, and I also want
   to be transparent — we have another strong candidate we need to respond to by [date].
   I'd love for it to be you. Is there one thing we could resolve today that would let
   you say yes?" This creates urgency without aggression.

Q17: What do I do if the hiring manager promised a number I can't honour in the offer?
A: Acknowledge the gap immediately and take ownership: "I owe you an apology — there
   was a miscommunication on our end, and the offer doesn't reflect what [manager] may
   have indicated. Let me explain what I can do and see if we can find a path forward."
   Never make the candidate feel they misheard. The error is yours to own.

Q18: How do I handle a candidate negotiating for a role they are overqualified for?
A: Be direct: "I want to make sure this role will keep you engaged in 12 months. Can
   we talk about what draws you to this level?" If their reasons are genuine (lifestyle,
   work-life balance), proceed. If they seem to be settling, flag it — a bored employee
   who leaves in 6 months costs more than a declined offer.

Q19: A candidate wants equity but we don't have an ESOP program. What do I offer instead?
A: Offer performance-linked long-term incentives (LTI): a cash bonus tied to company
   valuation milestones or profit sharing. Frame it as: "We don't have a formal ESOP
   yet, but here's how you participate in the company's growth financially." If the
   company is pre-ESOP, get a written commitment that the candidate will be in the
   first ESOP pool when it launches.

Q20: How do I negotiate a notice period buyout into the offer?
A: Make it a separate line item: "We'll cover up to ₹X as a notice period buyout,
   paid as a joining bonus against proof of your resignation letter and final settlement
   document." Cap it clearly and tie it to documentation — don't offer open-ended
   buyout commitments, as these can become expensive and contested.

Q21: The candidate wants to negotiate on work-from-home days. How do I handle this?
A: If policy is fixed, be clear upfront. If there's flexibility, use it as a lever:
   "I can't move on the base, but I can lock in 3 WFH days per week in writing in your
   offer letter — which is worth roughly ₹3,000–₹5,000/month in commute and time saved."
   WFH flexibility has real monetary value and high perceived value.

Q22: How do I re-engage a candidate who rejected our offer three months ago?
A: Reach out with something new: "I wanted to reconnect — since we last spoke, the role
   has evolved and we've revised the compensation band. Would you be open to a
   15-minute call?" Never just reopen the same offer. Show that something has changed —
   band revision, title change, or project scope — otherwise you signal desperation.

Q23: How do I negotiate with a candidate referred by a senior internal employee?
A: Treat them identically to any other candidate — no special salary commitments
   upfront. Brief the referring employee in advance: "We'll make a fair, market-aligned
   offer. I want to make sure [candidate] joins for the right reasons." Referral pressure
   on compensation leads to internal equity problems and resentment from peers.

Q24: A candidate is comparing our startup with a large MNC offer. How do I compete?
A: Don't try to match on fixed salary — compete on a different axis entirely. Lead with:
   ownership ("you'll run this function, not execute tasks"), speed ("you'll make
   decisions that would take 2 years to reach you at an MNC"), and wealth creation
   ("here's what your ESOP could be worth at our next funding round or exit"). The
   MNC candidate who joins a startup for salary rarely stays.

Q25: How do I handle a candidate who wants a salary higher than their future manager's?
A: This is an internal equity issue — flag it honestly: "I have to be transparent: this
   would create a compensation inversion with your reporting manager, which our policy
   doesn't allow. Here's what I can do structurally..." Options include a different
   reporting line, title adjustment, or deferred salary increase tied to manager's
   next review. Never create the inversion silently — it always surfaces and creates
   team conflict.

Q26: What should I do if a candidate accepts and then tries to renegotiate on joining day?
A: Hold firm with empathy: "I understand joining is a big step and feelings can shift.
   Our offer was agreed in writing and we've held the position for you. I'm not able
   to revise at this stage, but I want to make sure you're confident about the decision.
   What specific concern has come up?" Address the concern, not the number.

Q27: How do I negotiate with a candidate who has a higher salary than our entire team?
A: Be honest early: "I want to share our team's compensation context so you can make
   an informed decision — this role is structured at ₹X, which is below your current
   level. I don't want to waste your time if that's a hard constraint." If they proceed,
   document that they understand the pay structure. This prevents future resentment.

Q28: A candidate is asking for a sign-on bonus to cover their annual bonus forfeiture.
A: This is legitimate and common. Ask for documentation: "If you can share your last
   bonus letter or offer letter showing the forfeited amount, I'll build a business case
   to match up to 80% of that as a joining bonus." Cap it and tie it to a clawback
   clause: if they leave within 12 months, 50% is returned. This protects both sides.

Q29: How do I say no to a salary request without losing the candidate?
A: The formula: Validate → Explain → Offer an alternative → Invite dialogue.
   "I hear you — ₹X is a fair ask for your profile. Our ceiling for this band is ₹Y,
   and I can't go above that without a re-grade. What I can do is [title upgrade /
   early review / guaranteed bonus]. Would either of those make this work for you?"
   A 'no' with an alternative keeps the conversation alive. A bare 'no' ends it.

Q30: What are the top 5 mistakes new HR executives make in salary negotiations?
A: 1. Revealing the maximum budget too early — always start below your ceiling.
   2. Filling silence with concessions — silence is not rejection, it's thinking.
   3. Negotiating only on fixed salary — total comp has many more levers.
   4. Making verbal promises not in the offer letter — always put it in writing.
   5. Taking candidate pushback personally — it's a professional process, not a
      criticism of your judgment. Stay curious, not defensive.
